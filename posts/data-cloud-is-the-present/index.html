The Data Cloud is the Present

I have been thinking about this blog post a lot before I wrote it. I pined over how to communicate how data cloud platforms are the future, but ultimately I have decided to scrap that idea. They are not the future, because they are actually the present. Having a powerful data platform in the cloud you can pipeline all your Org’s data to is not a brand-new thing. It already exists, many Orgs are already doing it, and if you are reading this you should consider leveraging them too.

There are a plethora of IT specific data tools out there. A lot of them are some form of evolved log shipping tools that aggregate data to a centralized Sys Log Server, or something similar. These tools are fine, and they are better than not having any data pipelines or data tools, but IT and Operations shops don’t have to settle for IT specific data tools. IT and Operations teams can leverage all the same data cloud platforms that other teams use. Having all your data in a single platform is beyond powerful for IT. You will gain insights to your technology stacks, assets, users, software, and so much more by simply being able to join multiple sources of truth. You will have better data and leverage over your data than any single software tool can promise you.

So, the Data Cloud is not the future as it is currently the present. Other systems are now considered legacy as they cannot scale or offer some modern features the cloud can.

Being Data Driven and Data Enabled

Many organizations strive to be data-driven, and many humans have written books, done keynote presentations, built frameworks, and more around being data driven. Ultimately you can reduce it to the ability to make decisions based off of data you have collected, and then leveraged to ensure you are developing a solution or reach an end goal that meets a specific result. Data is consumed, analyzed, ran through a process, and that process ultimately leads some sort of desired end result. Many people and organizations are wanting to accomplish this, and many do.

However, being data driven is only part of the equation. You need to ensure your data is good. You need to have domain experts explain data, apply context to the data, and work with the data. This is where IT can be a huge help to an organization. IT is oftentimes the stewards of systems, and the custodians of technology stacks that organizations use. Enabling IT to leverage all and organization’s data, share data freely among teams, and training them on how to use these data tools will have exponential returns. This is what being data enabled means. Enable your IT teams to access data, train them to use the data tools, allow them to be subject-matter experts in their domain, and share data to them, they can leverage.

These are things I use in my every-day-work life now. My manager has a question for me, I write a query and ask the data. My team wants greater insights into a process, system or something else, we write a query and ask the data a question. Other teams want to know about IT systems, we enable them by sharing the data to them. When a security compliance adn governance engineer wants to build dashboards out of your IT Operations data to show how compliant your end user computing device fleet is, it is so much better of an experience if IT and Operations teams can just hand the compliance engineer a query. A compliance engineer is not a subject-matter expert in end user computers, IT is though. This way the data is more accurate and it drives in cross-team collaboration. This is what being data enabled means to me.

At a process level everyone in your Organization should strive to be data driven, and at an operational level everyone should strive to be data enabled.

Real World Example - Device Compliance

We have fine-tuned our data and queries to reflect the most real world data we can. Filtering out things that do not reflect a specific context, taking additional things into consideration, and using all the data we have to get the absolute most accurate results we can.

In the beginning things will look horrible, and this is normal. You never had all this data before, how could you have known every single edge case? How could you have anticipated everything? As Douglas Adams wrote, “Don’t Panic!” All this means is that you have an opportunity to vastly improve things adn the data is showing you all your gaps. This is also where IT can shine and bring real value to the organization and the data everyone leverages. It also gets better over time, like most things in life. In the beginning of most journeys it is the absolute most difficult and over time you gain experience and skills to mitigate that difficulty.

You will deal with number chasers, number chasers want good numbers when it comes to compliance. They oftentimes even ask for 100% compliance results, which is not easy at all. This is where context matters, and it can matter a lot. One of the filters I put into place was exited employees. Most orgs have some sort of off boarding process, and that typically also involves putting their computer into some sort of hold state. This means their Org provided laptop could be locked up in storage somewhere for some amount of time. This also means it is not online and receiving patches or updates. So, we decided to filter these systems out of our main data sets of production laptops. We never get rid of data either, we filter them into buckets with applied context. We still care about the device, but now it is going into another bucket of data with a different context applied to it. We also filtered out any non-in-use system. So, systems that are out for repairs, or in stock and ready to be deployed, designated demo systems, digital signage, conference room computers, so on and so forth also get their own context.

Now our numbers looked a lot better to those number chaser folks, and they were actually more accurate under the context of production employee systems. By design a computer powering the audio/video systems in a conference room doesn’t have any access to any crown jewels data, nor assumes any roles within privileged systems, and really is just sort of a dummy appliance computer that helps a larger conference room run the hardware with in it. Again, we still care about these systems, we still report on them, but they just go to different dashboards with different applied context to them.
